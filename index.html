1. About

My PhD work is a first-author paper in the Journal of Neuroscience (in press). I discovered that a single gene (Adgrl2) uses alternative splicing to play completely different roles in neurons versus blood vessels. I found the pattern computationally in single-cell RNA-seq across 100K+ cells, then validated it myself at the bench through mouse models, BBB permeability assays, and confocal imaging. Both sides of the paper are mine: the computational pipeline and the wet lab validation.

Outside the lab, I've built two production data systems from scratch: a system that predicts how humans will rank LLMs using only benchmark scores1, and a fully automated daily news podcast that has published 475+ episodes to YouTube.2

I design end-to-end pipelines, from data collection through statistical modeling through deliverable output. I'm finishing my PhD in June 2026 and looking for roles in computational biology, data science, or applied AI where rigorous analysis drives real decisions.

2. Projects

PhD Research: Adgrl2 Alternative Splicing in Cerebrovasculature

Discovered that Adgrl2 uses cell-type-specific alternative splicing to serve distinct roles in neurons (synapse assembly) versus endothelial cells (blood-brain barrier integrity). Built end-to-end R/Python pipelines for single-cell RNA-seq analysis across 100K+ cells, then validated computationally identified splicing patterns at the bench through conditional knockouts, isoform swap experiments, BBB permeability assays, and confocal imaging.

Python R scRNA-seq Scanpy Seurat Confocal

Project Soothsayer: LLM Evaluation & Prediction System

End-to-end system that predicts human preference ELO scores from benchmark data alone. The core challenge: only 123 training examples, 65+ features, and ~40% missing data. Built custom per-cell imputation with SVD coherence projection, designed 4 original LLM benchmarks, and ran 50+ controlled experiments. R² = 0.93, near-perfect rank ordering (Spearman ρ = 0.966), predictions within 1.3% of actual scores.

Polygenic Disease Gene Prediction

Built multi-omic networks (~65K nodes, ~7.3M edges) linking DNA, RNA, and protein layers to predict how disease-associated genes converge on common processes. Graph-based semi-supervised learning to trace GWAS risk loci to microglial activation and synaptic signaling. Co-first-author paper in BMC Bioinformatics.
