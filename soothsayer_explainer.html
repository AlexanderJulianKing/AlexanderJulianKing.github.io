Project Soothsayer

LLM Evaluation & Arena ELO Prediction

A multi-benchmark evaluation suite that predicts human preference before anyone votes. Four custom benchmarks join 11 public leaderboards, feed through a per-cell imputation engine, and emerge as ELO predictions within 0.9% of actual scores.

Why does this exist?

The Problem

Arena.ai captures something no benchmark can—real human preference, derived from millions of blind head-to-head votes. But what it measures is difficult to pin down. It isn’t model intelligence in any strict sense, and it isn’t objective quality. It reflects what people prefer, which is a fundamentally different signal. Traditional benchmarks don’t measure it, and it’s not clear they can.

Why It’s Hard

Benchmarks evaluate well-defined capabilities: coding, mathematics, logical reasoning. Human preference is nearly a black box. Intelligence is multidimensional and context-dependent—the gap between “this model performs well” and “people prefer this model” is real and nontrivial. Compounding this, the benchmark matrix is roughly 40% sparse—not every model is evaluated on every test.

5. From Features to Predictions

An adaptive nearest-neighbor approach that accounts for how human preference works differently at different competency levels.

The Key Finding

The same benchmark can predict Arena scores in opposite directions depending on LLM quality. For example, list formatting positively predicts human preference for mid-tier LLMs (ρ=+0.16) but negatively predicts it for top-tier LLMs (ρ=−0.74). A global score prediction model averages these effects to near zero. Instead, we need an approach that adapts to the local regime.

1.

Adaptive KNN with Sublinear Power Cutoff

For each model, find its nearest neighbors in the 105-dimensional benchmark space. The neighborhood radius follows a sublinear power law: max_dist = dnearest0.7 × 3. This naturally gives tighter neighborhoods in dense regions (top models, k≈25) while keeping adequate coverage in sparse regions—ensuring the local regression stays within a consistent feature-preference regime.

2.

Kernel-Weighted Ridge Regression

Within each neighborhood, fit a weighted Ridge regression where closer neighbors have more influence (Gaussian kernel). The regularization strength adapts to the neighborhood’s score spread. This learns which benchmarks matter for this specific group of models—not a global average.

3.

Jackknife Bias Correction

Ridge shrinks predictions toward the neighborhood mean. The jackknife leaves each neighbor out, refits, and measures how much Ridge compresses. This compression slope (typically 1.1–1.5×) is used to inflate the prediction back outward, correcting the centering bias.

Adaptive Neighborhoods

The cutoff radius is dnearest0.7 × 3—sublinear in nearest-neighbor distance. Distinctive LLMs (few close peers in feature space) get a small d0 and a tight neighborhood; models that cluster together share a small d0 too, but many peers fall within that tight radius, so k is large. Each neighborhood becomes its own private training set for a kernel-weighted ridge regression—with completely different dominant features depending on the regime.
